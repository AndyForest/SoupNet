# Serendipity, Diversity, and Novelty Metrics — Evidence Memo

Research memo for the check-recipe ranking system (docs/planning/check-recipe-ranking-system.md §5.4).
Topic: candidate operationalizations of "utility × surprise" as an offline metric for the regression
harness. Constraint: computable from embeddings + golden relevance labels only — no click logs, no
truncation (reorder only). Researched 2026-07-16.

## 1. Serendipity in the literature: unexpectedness × relevance

The consensus decomposition is that serendipity = relevance + novelty + unexpectedness. The
canonical survey is Kotkov, Wang & Veijalainen, ["A survey of serendipity in recommender
systems"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0950705116302763) (Knowledge-Based
Systems, 2016): serendipity "reflects how good a recommender system is at suggesting serendipitous
items that are relevant, novel and unexpected," where novelty (user hasn't seen it) is distinguished
from unexpectedness (user wouldn't have looked there). (UNVERIFIED at verbatim level — paywalled;
wording via [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/cf9c0846c6b1c97b89fb27d5acef6db18fe04039)
and the [Ziarani & Ravanmehr systematic review, JCST 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11390-020-0135-9).)

The classic computable formulation is Ge, Delgado-Battenfeld & Jannach, ["Beyond accuracy:
evaluating recommender systems by coverage and serendipity"](https://web-ainf.aau.at/pub/jannach/files/Conference_RecSys2010.pdf)
(RecSys 2010), building on Murakami et al.: unexpectedness is defined against a *primitive baseline
recommender*, then multiplied by usefulness:

> "The metric proposed in this paper is intended to catch the two essential aspects of serendipity,
> unexpectedness and usefulness."
> "Let PM be a set of recommendations generated by a primitive prediction model and RS denote the
> recommendations generated by a recommender system. When an element of RS does not belong to PM,
> we consider the element is an unexpected recommendation. … UNEXP = RS \ PM"
> — Ge et al., RecSys 2010

SRDP is then the mean usefulness u(RSᵢ) over UNEXP. **Critical degenerate case for us:** SRDP is a
*set-difference* metric. In a no-truncation system, RS and PM contain the same items in different
orders, so UNEXP = ∅ and SRDP is identically zero. Any Ge-style metric here must be rebuilt on *rank
displacement* against the pure-cosine baseline rather than set membership (§5, Candidate B).

## 2. Surprise from embeddings alone (no baseline system, no logs)

Kaminskas & Bridge, ["Measuring Surprise in Recommender
Systems"](http://www.cs.ucc.ie/~dgb/papers/Kaminskas-Bridge-2014.pdf) (RecSys 2014 workshop) is the
key paper for our setting: surprise as pure geometry, "without comparing them against an alternative
system." They adopt "the idea of measuring an item's distance from the user's profile" and argue
**min-distance beats average-distance**:

> "Intuitively, a user perceives a recommendation as surprising if it is sufficiently different from
> any item the user has seen before. This information may be lost when averaging the computed
> distances, especially if the user has been exposed to diverse items. Therefore, we propose using
> the lower bound item distance from the user's profile items as an indicator of surprise."
> "Scont(i) = min dist(i, j)" [over profile items j]
> — Kaminskas & Bridge, RecSys 2014

They deliberately leave relevance out ("we leave it to be measured separately by accuracy metrics"),
whereas Adamopoulos & Tuzhilin (per the same paper) fold both into one objective: "maximizing a
utility function which combines an item's relevance and its distance from a set of 'expected'
items. The set of expected items includes all items rated (i.e., seen) by the user and items similar
to the seen ones." That "expected set" construction maps directly onto our query embedding +
`known_recipes` + the echo-suppression context.

Vargas & Castells, ["Rank and Relevance in Novelty and Diversity Metrics for Recommender
Systems"](https://castells.github.io/papers/recsys2011.pdf) (RecSys 2011) is the unifying framework:
every metric is a rank-discounted, relevance-weighted sum of a per-item novelty model. Two novelty
models matter:

- **Popularity-based** (EPC — expected popularity complement): "high novelty values correspond to
  long-tail items few users have interacted with." *Not computable for us* — we have no meaningful
  interaction-popularity signal (feedback rows are too sparse/young to stand in).
- **Distance-based** (EPD — expected profile distance): "we consider a similarity-based model where
  item novelty is defined by a distance function between the item and a context of experience" —
  fully computable from embeddings. Kaminskas & Bridge note EPD is their surprise idea under another
  name ("the authors do not use the term 'serendipity', but present it as a personalized novelty
  metric").

Their diversity metric EILD adds rank and relevance to plain ILD: "Equation 16 thus generalizes the
average intra-list distance (ILD) [20,22] with the introduction of rank-sensitivity and relevance."
They also show empirically why a *single integrated metric* beats averaging separate accuracy and
novelty numbers: a harmonic mean of nDCG and EPC "fails to realize that this item is not relevant …
In contrast, EPCrank,rel does not suffer from these shortcomings."

## 3. Diversity and coverage

- **ILD** (Ziegler et al. 2005 lineage): average pairwise distance between list items — trivially
  computable from result embeddings.
- **Coverage** (Ge et al. 2010): "catalogue coverage … the percentage of the available items which
  effectively are ever recommended to a user," measured "on a set of recommendation sessions." For
  us: fraction of corpus surfaced as *exemplars* across the whole golden query set — a natural
  stigmergy-health metric (does the display layer ever show the long tail?).
- **α-nDCG / subtopic-aware metrics** (Clarke et al., SIGIR 2008) penalize redundancy given subtopic
  labels; our k-means cluster assignments can proxy subtopics. (UNVERIFIED — not fetched this
  session; standard IR result.)

## 4. Trade-offs and measurement pitfalls

1. **Serendipity trades against accuracy by construction.** Ge et al.: "the more we focus on
   increasing accuracy by yielding catalog coverage, the less serendipity happens," and "even a
   'perfect' content-based recommender cannot find surprising items." Kaminskas & Bridge validated
   offline that "a matrix factorization approach generates the most accurate but also the least
   surprising recommendations." Report the *pair* (relevance metric, serendipity metric), never a
   pre-blended scalar with hidden weights.
2. **ILD is gameable and pathological.** Ohsaka & Togashi, ["A Critical Reexamination of Intra-List
   Distance and Dispersion"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13801) (SIGIR 2023): "ILD may select
   duplicate items that are very close to each other, whereas dispersion may overlook distant item
   pairs" — i.e., a list of two tight far-apart clumps maximizes ILD while feeling redundant. They
   propose Gaussian ILD (a bandwidth-tunable interpolation between ILD and min-pairwise dispersion).
3. **Embedding-distance diversity must be validated per domain.** Jesse, Bauer & Jannach,
   ["Intra-list similarity and human diversity perceptions of recommendations: the details
   matter"](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11257-022-09351-w) (UMUAI 2023), four user
   studies: "while we found that the ILS metric *can* be a good proxy for human perceptions, it
   turns out that it is important to individually validate the used ILS metric implementation for a
   given application." Latent-vector ILS worked for movies and failed for recipes (food recipes —
   the coincidence is instructive). Our analog validation signal is the structured feedback rows
   (impact/disposition/story_fulfilled), not clicks — periodically correlate the offline metric
   against them.
4. **Min vs. avg is a real choice, not a detail.** The two surprise variants in Kaminskas & Bridge
   ranked items with near-zero Spearman correlation across their two definitions — pick one,
   document it, and keep it stable in the harness.
5. **Offline serendipity scores are proxies.** Ge et al.'s own validation design was a user study;
   practitioner writeups ([Shaped](https://www.shaped.ai/blog/not-your-average-recsys-metrics-part-1-serendipity))
   likewise stress verifying "through user feedback and other evaluation metrics." Goodhart applies:
   the harness metric is for *regression detection and tuning comparison*, not a north star to
   maximize unboundedly.

## 5. Recommendations for this system

All inputs exist today: query embedding **q**, result embeddings **eᵢ**, display order (cluster
ordering + exemplar position) rank(i), cluster assignments c(i), golden graded relevance labels
rel_g(i) ∈ [0,1] per golden question, and optionally the golden question's `known_recipes` set K.

**Candidate A — Expected Serendipity (primary; EPD × relevance, Vargas-scheme):**

    unexp(i) = min over x ∈ E of cosdist(e_i, x),  E = {q} ∪ embeddings(K)
    Ser@L    = (1/Z) · Σ_i disc(rank(i)) · rel_g(i) · unexp(i),   disc(k) = 1/log2(k+1)
    Z        = Σ_i disc(rank(i))   (normalizer so lists of different lengths compare)

Utility × surprise per item, exactly Andy's stated objective; min-distance per Kaminskas & Bridge;
rank/relevance integration per Vargas & Castells. Pure cosine ranking scores low (top items have
unexp ≈ 0); random reorder also scores low (high-unexp items have rel_g ≈ 0). The maximum lives at
"distant enough to teach, applicable enough to act on."

**Candidate B — Serendipitous rank-gain (baseline-relative; Ge/Murakami rebuilt for reorder-only):**

    gain(i)  = max(0, rank_cos(i) − rank_new(i)) / N        (normalized promotion vs pure cosine)
    SerGain  = Σ_i rel_g(i) · unexp(i) · gain(i)  /  Σ_i rel_g(i) · unexp(i)

"Of the serendipity mass available in the retrieved set, how much did the display layer surface?"
Zero for the cosine baseline by construction — ideal for A/B-ing ranking variants in the harness and
for proving the cluster-layer change moves something.

**Candidate C — Guardrail pair (report alongside A/B, never blended):**

    nDCG@L over rel_g (relevance guardrail)  +  aspect coverage: |{c(i) : rank(i) ≤ L, rel_g(i) > 0}| / |clusters with any relevant item|

Plus, corpus-wide per harness run: exemplar catalog coverage (Ge et al.) — fraction of corpus ever
shown as an exemplar across the golden query set. If plain ILD is wanted for dashboards, prefer
Gaussian ILD or report dispersion (min pairwise distance) beside it per Ohsaka & Togashi.

Suggested adoption order: A as the headline regression metric, B for ranking-variant comparisons, C
always printed next to both. Revalidate A's unexp() against accumulated feedback rows quarterly
(Jesse et al. pitfall).
